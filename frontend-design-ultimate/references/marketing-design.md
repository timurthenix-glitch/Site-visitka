# Marketing Design Reference

Use this reference when building:
- Landing pages and marketing sites
- Promotional materials and announcements
- Creative artifacts and posters
- Public-facing pages meant to impress
- Any interface where first impression matters most

## Marketing Design Principles

### First Impression Is Everything

Users decide whether to stay in milliseconds. The above-the-fold experience must:
- Communicate value immediately (headline + subheadline)
- Show, don't just tell (visual proof, not feature lists)
- Create emotional response (the feeling from your intent loop)
- Guide to a single primary action

### Narrative Flow

Marketing pages tell a story. Structure matters:
1. **Hook:** Why should they care? (Hero)
2. **Problem:** What pain do they feel? (Context)
3. **Solution:** How do you fix it? (Features)
4. **Proof:** Why believe you? (Social proof, testimonials, data)
5. **Action:** What should they do now? (CTA)

Not every page needs all sections. Match the story to the user's awareness level.

### Typography for Impact

- Headlines can be huge. Marketing is where display typefaces shine.
- Contrast creates drama: huge headline + small body, or light weight + bold weight.
- Line-height can be tight for headlines (1.0–1.1) and generous for body (1.6–1.8).
- Readability still matters — don't sacrifice legibility for style.

### Color for Emotion

- Marketing pages can be more expressive than apps. Saturated colors, gradients, and textures are appropriate.
- But every color must serve the narrative. "Blue because trustworthy" is default. "Deep ocean blue because we're about depth and exploration" is intent.
- Dark mode can be dramatic and premium. Light mode can be airy and approachable.

### Motion for Delight

- Entrance animations should feel choreographed, not random.
- Stagger reveals: elements appear in sequence, not all at once.
- Scroll-triggered animations should enhance understanding, not distract.
- Hover states can be playful and surprising.
- Page load should feel like an event.

### Spatial Composition

- Break the grid. Overlap elements. Use asymmetric layouts.
- Generous whitespace signals confidence and premium quality.
- Full-bleed sections create immersion.
- Diagonal flow and curved dividers add energy.

### Social Proof

- Logos of recognizable brands build trust immediately.
- Testimonials are most effective with photos, names, and specific outcomes.
- Numbers are compelling when specific ("10,000+" is weaker than "12,847")
- Case studies should tell a before/after story

### CTAs

- Primary CTA should be visually dominant and consistently placed.
- Copy should be action-oriented ("Start building" > "Submit")
- Secondary CTAs should be visually subordinate but still accessible.
- Repeat the primary CTA at the bottom of long pages.

## Marketing Anti-Patterns

- Hero sections that don't communicate value ("Welcome to our platform")
- Feature lists without benefit statements
- Testimonials without attribution or photos
- Generic stock photography
- CTA copy like "Learn More" or "Click Here"
- Infinite scroll without footer access
- Auto-playing video with sound
- Popups before the user has engaged with content
- Cookie banners that block the entire viewport
- "Trusted by" logos that are unrecognizable or fake

## Poster / Artifact Design

For creative artifacts, posters, and one-off visual pieces:
- **One big idea.** A poster should communicate one thing powerfully.
- **Hierarchy is extreme.** One element dominates. Everything else supports.
- **Negative space is active.** The empty areas shape the composition as much as the filled ones.
- **Type as image.** Letters can be the visual, not just the message.
- **Color as emotion.** A poster can be monochrome, duotone, or wildly polychromatic — but it must be intentional.
- **Texture adds humanity.** Grain, paper texture, ink bleed, halftone patterns.

## Marketing → App Handoff

If you're building both marketing site and app:
- Marketing can be expressive; app should be efficient.
- Shared tokens (colors, typography) create cohesion, but density and motion differ.
- The transition from marketing to app should feel like entering the product, not visiting a different website.
